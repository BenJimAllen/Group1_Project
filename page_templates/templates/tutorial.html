Hidden Pair

(Feldman)

The 5 and 6 cannot occupy any of the highlighted cells. Therefore, they must be placed in the remaining two uncolored cells.

(Feldman)

One of the two possible placements for 5 and 6. A later clue will reveal which of the outcomes is part of the solution.

The Hidden Pair technique is a Sudoku strategy that helps to identify pairs of numbers that must be placed in specific cells. The method extends the Hidden Single technique, which focuses on finding a single number that can only be placed in one cell.

Here is how the Hidden Pair technique works:

  1. Identify a region (row, column, or 3x3 block of cells) with only a few empty cells.
  2. Look for two numbers that must be placed in two specific cells within the selected region, but their exact positions are unknown.
  3. Eliminate all other candidates from those two cells, leaving only the hidden pair.

For example, if you find that 5 and 6 can only be placed in two specific cells within a block, and their exact positions are unknown, you have discovered a hidden pair. You can then eliminate all other candidates from those two cells, leaving only 5 and 6.

To spot hidden pairs effectively, prioritize the regions that have few empty cells and those for which many cells can be ruled out at once. Combining the search for hidden singles, hidden pairs, and even larger hidden groups can help you to spot these patterns more easily.

Remember that for the technique to work, there must be only two cells where the two candidates may go. If you find three cells for two numbers, this is not a hidden pair.

The strength of finding a hidden pair lies not only in knowing that the numbers are in those cells, but also in the fact that all other numbers cannot be there, which helps further narrow down the possibilities in the puzzle.

Locked Candidate

(Feldman)

Line-to-Block Reduction : The 2 in the lower-middle block limits the placement of a 2 to two specific cells within the upper-right block.

(Feldman)

Block-to-Line Reduction : The 5 in the lower-right block limits the placement of a 5 to two specific cells within the upper-middle block.

The Locked Candidate technique is a Sudoku solving strategy that focuses on candidates that are restricted within an intersecting region between a 3x3 block of cells and a row or column. It helps to eliminate candidates from other cells in the same block, row, or column. There are two types of Locked Candidate techniques: Line-to-Block Reduction and Block-to-Line Reduction.

Line-to-Block Reduction : In this technique, a specific number is restricted to the intersection of a block and a line (row or column). Consequently, the number cannot appear elsewhere in the block. To apply this technique, do the following:

  1. Identify a number that is restricted within the intersection of a block and a line.
  2. Eliminate the number as a candidate from the other cells within the block.

Block-to-Line Reduction : Also known as Pointing Pairs or Pointing Triples, this technique involves restricting a specific number within the intersection of a block and a line so that the number cannot appear elsewhere in the line. To apply this technique, do the following:

  1. Identify a number that is restricted within the intersection of a block and a line.
  2. Eliminate the number as a candidate from the other cells within the line.

To find Locked Candidates effectively, start looking for them when there are no singles or groups left to find. Look for regions where many cells can be affected at once, so that a number is squeezed into a block, row, or column.

X-Wing

(Feldman)

The cells that are highlighted in green make up the X-Wing. The candidates that are highlighted in red can be eliminated.

(Feldman)

An X-Wing that is completely contained within a single block is not possible because there would be a repeated number in that block.

The X-Wing technique is a strategy that is used to eliminate candidates in Sudoku.

An X-Wing occurs when a specific number appears as a candidate in exactly two cells within two different rows, and these cells are also aligned in the same columns. The name X-Wing comes from the X-shaped pattern formed by the cells when they are connected.

The essential idea behind the X-Wing technique is that since the number can only appear in one of the two cells in each row, it must also appear in one of the two cells in the corresponding columns. Thus, the number cannot appear in any other cells within those columns.

To apply the X-Wing technique, do the following:

  1. Identify two rows where a specific number appears as a candidate in exactly two cells, and the cells are aligned in the same columns.
  2. Recognize that the number will eventually be placed in one of the two cells in each row, and also in one of the two cells in each column.
  3. Eliminate the number as a candidate from any other cells within the columns.

The X-Wing technique can also be applied in the opposite direction, where the restriction is given by columns, and the candidates can be eliminated from rows.

If a number is restricted within columns, an X-Wing allows you to eliminate from rows; if a number is restricted within rows, an X-Wing allows you to eliminate from columns.

Swordfish

(Feldman)

The only distinction between the Swordfish technique and the X-Wing technique is that the configuration of a Swordfish is slightly more difficult to identify because it is composed of nine cells rather than four cells.

(Feldman)

The cells that are highlighted in green and yellow make up the Swordfish. The candidates that are highlighted in red can be eliminated.

The Swordfish technique is is an advanced Sudoku solving strategy that builds on the X-Wing technique. Whereas an X-Wing consists of a 2x2 matrix of cells, the Swordfish technique works with a 3x3 matrix of cells. A Swordfish is also called a 3x3 Fish.

The Swordfish technique works by identifying a specific number that appears in exactly three cells in each of the three rows or columns, forming a 3x3 matrix of cells. The configuration allows you to eliminate the number as a candidate from the other cells in the intersecting rows or columns.

To apply the Swordfish technique, do the following:

  1. Identify a number that appears in exactly three cells in each of the three rows or columns that form a 3x3 matrix of cells.
  2. Visualize or mark the 3x3 matrix, making note of the instersecting rows and columns.
  3. Eliminate the identified number as a candidate from the other cells within the intersecting rows or columns.

The Swordfish technique still works even if some of the candidates have been eliminated from the 3x3 matrix, which makes it an incomplete Swordfish. As long as the remaining configuration still follows the rules of the Swordfish technique, you can eliminate the candidate from the intersecting rows or columns.

Essentially, the Swordfish technique helps you to rule out candidates by identifying a specific number that is restricted within a 3x3 matrix of rows or columns. The technique can be more challenging to use when compared to the X-Wing, but it is nevertheless a powerful tool for solving advanced Sudoku puzzles.

Jellyfish

(Feldman)

The Jellyfish technique functions similarly to both the X-Wing and Swordfish techniques.

(Feldman)

The cells that are highlighted in blue make up the complementary Column-to-Row Jellyfish.

The Jellyfish technique is is an advanced Sudoku solving strategy and a 4x4 variant of the X-Wing technique. Similar to the X-Wing and Swordfish techniques, the Jellyfish technique helps you to eliminate candidates in rows or columns, depending on the particular configuration.

In a Jellyfish, four candidates appear in exactly four rows (or columns) and are aligned in such a way that they also occupy only four columns (or rows). The technique is based on the principle that each of the four candidates must be placed in one of the four intersecting cells in their respective rows or columns.

To apply the Jellyfish technique, do the following:

  1. Look for a candidate that is confined to four columns in four selected rows.
  2. If you find a Jellyfish configuration, you can eliminate the candidate from any other cells in the involved columns (or rows) outside of the Jellyfish.

In standard 9x9 Sudoku puzzles, you only need to look for X-Wings (2x2), Swordfish (3x3), and Jellyfish (4x4). For every fish that is 5x5 or larger, there would always be a complementary fish that is smaller in size, so you do not need to look for the bigger one.

Interestingly, Jellyfish often appear to have complementary Jellyfish, where a Row-to-Column Jellyfish is accompanied by a Column-to-Row Jellyfish. The pattern is quite common in Sudoku puzzles featuring Jellyfish configurations.
